What is accessibility?

Making great products

When you make your products and services accessible, you make them usable for everybody.

Making your products and services accessible means thinking about accessibility from inception to production, and every point in between.

Making great products

Products and services include:

  • Websites and webapps
  • Apps and applications
  • Documents
  • Video games and XR spaces
  • Smart speaker skills and actions

Making great products

Accessibility does not mean limited design or text-only content.

The BBC Weather apps for iOS and Android have lots of features and a design to match, and they were launched with full accessibility.

Accessibility is great design!

Mark of quality

When products and services are accessible, it's a mark of quality.

When you make accessibility a core design principle for your products and services, it turns them into great user experiences for everyone.

Mark of quality

Making your products and services accessible is a matter of professional pride.

You should not consider that your job is done unless you've considered accessibility at every stage.

Reducing risk

Accessibility is a legal requirement in many countries, including:

  • Equality Act; UK
  • Disability Discrimination Act; Northern Ireland
  • Americans with Disabilities Act (ADA); USA
  • Rehabilitation Act, Section 508; USA
  • Disability Discrimination Act; Australia
  • Ontarians with Disabilities Accessibility Act (AODA); Canada
  • Accessibility Act; European Union (EU)
  • Public Sector Accessibility Directive; EU

Reducing risk

You may be familiar with some of the brands that have been successfully sued for failing to make their products and services accessible:

  • Domino's was sued in 2021 by Guillermo Robles, a blind person who was unable to order pizza using their app or website
  • Beyoncé’s company Parkwood entertainment was sued in 2019 by Mary Conner Fin, a blind person who was unable to purchase concert tickets from the star's website
  • Netflix was sued in 2012 by the National Association of the Deaf in the US, after the streaming giant refused a request to caption their video content

Reducing risk

Most legal cases are brought in the USA because its laws are favourable, and the financial penalties imposed on organisations found to be in breach of those laws are substantial.

Even though fewer cases reach court in other countries, significant damage can be done to an organisation's reputation if the threat of a case is reported in the press.

Maximising opportunity

When your products and services are accessible it maximises your opportunities to:

  • Reach more people
  • Grow your target audience
  • Create positive brand reputation
  • Generate strong brand loyalty
  • Increase the diversity of your teams

Maximising opportunity

In 2020, the World Health Organisation reported that 15% of the global population have a disability.

We know this is an underestimate, because it only includes documented disabilities like being blind or using a wheelchair, it does not include cognitive disabilities, reading and literacy difficulties, age-related disabilities, or many others.

Even a 15% increase in your target audience is worth it though!

Maximising opportunity

In 2020 the UK Government reported that:

  • 22% of people have a disability
  • 19% of working age people have a disability
  • 46% of people over 65 have a disability

If you make your products and services accessible, you could increase your target audience by as much as 46%!

Maximising opportunity

In 2020 the US Centers for Disease Control and Prevention (CDC) reported that:

  • 1 in 4 adults have a disability
  • 2 in 5 people over the age of 65 have a disability
  • 1 in 4 women have a disability
  • 2 in 5 non-Hispanic American Indians/ Alaska Natives have a disability

If you make your products and services accessible, you could increase your target audience by as much as 26%!

Maximising opportunity

When you make your products and services accessible, you help to generate a positive brand reputation for your organisation.

People with disabilities are often loyal to brands that get accessibility right, because they have a good experience when using their products or services.

People with disabilities also recommend accessible brands to friends and family, so the impact of accessibility goes further than you might think.

Doing the right thing

When products and services are accessible, it means that people can independently:

  • Look after their health
  • Connect with other people
  • Manage their finances
  • Buy clothes, groceries, and gifts
  • Get an education
  • Do their job

Doing the right thing

You're responsible for making your products and services accessible, and for enabling disabled people to choose the product or service they want to use because of its features (not just because it's the only accessible option available).

Doing the right thing

When you make your products and services accessible, you're helping to change these perceptions:

  • 73% of disabled people and 66% of the public think that disabled people are unable to live full and independent lives
  • 90% of disabled people and 86% of the public think disabled people do not have access to the education they need to thrive in society
  • 95% of disabled people and 92% of the public think that disabled people do not have access to the employment opportunities needed to thrive in society
  • 92% of disabled people and 88% of the public think that views held by the public are not helpful to disabled people
